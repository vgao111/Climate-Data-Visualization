About This Visualization

Design Rationale

We chose a choropleth world map as the entry point because warming is inherently a geographic phenomenon — readers immediately recognize their own country and can compare regions at a glance. A sequential yellow-to-red color scale (d3.interpolateYlOrRd) mirrors the intuitive "heat" metaphor: hotter colors mean more warming. We bounded the scale at 0–3°C because nearly every country falls in that range and it preserves visual contrast where it matters.

For interaction, we considered a single static map, a globe (3D rotation), and a small-multiples grid. We rejected the static map because it can't show how a country got to its current anomaly. We rejected the globe because rotating to find a country adds friction without insight. Small multiples would have worked but doesn't let users compare specific time periods. The map → drill-down design gives readers an overview first, then a deep dive on demand — a pattern Shneiderman calls "overview, zoom and filter, details-on-demand."

We added a year-range slider on the map itself so viewers can ask their own questions: "Was the world already warming in 1950?" or "Which countries warmed first?" Rather than freezing the comparison period at 2000–2014, the slider re-colors the entire map in real time. The country detail view layers an annual line, a 10-year rolling mean, and a decadal bar chart so users can see year-to-year noise, long-term trend, and decadal acceleration in one place.

For visual appeal, we added a zoom animation when a country is clicked: the map smoothly zooms toward the country before transitioning to its detail view, giving readers a clear sense of moving "into" a place rather than jumping to an unrelated page. We chose a light, traditional-map aesthetic (light-blue ocean, soft grid lines) because climate is a serious topic and we wanted the data, not the chrome, to feel like the subject.

Development Process

Team contributions. One member led the data pipeline (CMIP6 zarr loading, regionmask integration, country aggregation, and JSON export). The other led the front-end (D3 map, detail charts, interaction logic, and styling). Both members collaborated on choosing the question, the encoding, and the write-up.

Time spent. Roughly 30–35 people-hours total. The breakdown:

• ~8 hours understanding the dataset — CMIP6 is stored in zarr on Google Cloud and has non-trivial conventions (Kelvin, cftime calendars, 0–360° longitude, unequal grid-cell areas). Just getting a clean global-mean time series was the first hurdle.
• ~6 hours on exploratory plots (matplotlib + Plotly) to find a question worth asking. Polar amplification and the spread of country-level warming pointed us at the world-map idea.
• ~10 hours on the country-level aggregation — area-weighting with areacella, building the regionmask country grid, and resolving the dozens of name mismatches between regionmask's Natural Earth full names and the GeoJSON's short names (e.g. "United States of America" vs "USA").
• ~8 hours on the D3 visualization itself — map, color scale, tooltips, drill-down view, sliders, and the zoom animation.
• ~3 hours on styling, the write-up, and final polish.

Hardest part. Country name matching, by far. The CMIP6 data lives on a 192×288 grid that doesn't know about country boundaries; regionmask rasterizes Natural Earth polygons onto that grid using one naming convention, while the D3 GeoJSON we display uses a different one. After several rounds of console-debugging missing countries, we built a bidirectional alias dictionary in Python so every country in the visible map gets data. Small islands (Vatican, Singapore) still go uncolored because they're smaller than a single 1°×1° grid cell — an honest limitation of climate-model resolution, not a bug.
