About This Visualization

Design Rationale

We chose a choropleth world map as the entry point because warming is inherently a geographic phenomenon — readers immediately recognize their own country and can compare regions at a glance. This allows to user to recognize the impact on a global scale and also at a local scale, the individual countries. A binned yellow-to-red color scale (d3.interpolateYlOrRd) mirrors the intuitive idea where hotter colors mean more warming. We bounded the scale at 0–3°C because nearly every country falls in that range and it preserves visual contrast where it matters.

For interaction, we considered a single static map, a globe and a small-multiples grid. We rejected the static map because it can't show how a country got to its current anomaly. We rejected the globe because rotating to find a country adds complexity in otherwise should be a straightforward visualization.

We added a year-range slider on the map itself so viewers can ask their own questions: "Was the world already warming in 1950?" or "Which countries warmed first?" Rather than freezing the comparison period at 2000–2014, the slider re-colors the entire map in real time. The country detail view layers an annual line, a 10-year rolling mean, and a decadal bar chart so users can see year-to-year noise, long-term trend, and decadal acceleration in one place.

For visual appeal, we added a zoom animation when a country is clicked, the map smoothly zooms toward the country before transitioning to its detail view, giving readers a clear sense of moving "into" a place rather than jumping to an unrelated page. We chose a light, traditional-map aesthetic (light-blue ocean, soft grid lines) because climate is a serious topic and we wanted the data, not the chrome, to feel like the subject.

Development Process

Time spent. Roughly 25 people-hours total. The breakdown:

• ~All group members - 4 hours understanding the dataset — The CMIP6 dataset is stored in zarr on Google Cloud and has non-trivial conventions (Kelvin, cftime calendars, 0–360° longitude, unequal grid-cell areas). So really understanding the structure and how to access the data was challenging in itself.
• ~ All group members - 4 hours on exploratory plots (matplotlib + Plotly) to find a question worth asking. The pattern of individual country level warming compared to a universal baseline time pointed us at the world-map idea.
• ~ All group members - 6 hours on the country-level aggregation like area-weighting with areacella, building the regionmask country grid, and resolving the dozens of name mismatches between regionmask's Natural Earth full names and the GeoJSON's short names (e.g. "United States of America" vs "USA").
• ~ All group members - 6 hours on the D3 visualization itself: map, color scale, tooltips, drill-down view, sliders, and the zoom animation.
• ~ All group members - 5 hours on styling, the write-up, and final polish.

Most time consuming. Country name matching, by far. After several rounds of console-debugging missing countries, we built an alias dictionary in Python so every country in the visible map gets data. Small islands (Vatican, Singapore) still go uncolored because they're smaller than a single 1°×1° grid cell.
